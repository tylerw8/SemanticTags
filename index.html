Schooling

Grade School

I was born in Ogden, Utah on June 30, 1969. Just a few days before man first stepped foot on the moon. My first memory of a computer was in elementary school. Some college students came to my class to demostrate a lemonade stand game. It was text based and they didn't allow the kids to spend too much time playing with it.

In Junior High I learned to type. In my typing class we had six computers with word processing programs on them that students took turns using. I really liked being able to easily correct my typos without using whiteout! I also took a programming class that use IBM PET computers. I remember making an ASCII rocket fly off the screen, but that's about it.

I took another programming class in Basic in highschool. The main thing I recall from that class is the excitement over computers that could produce "High-Rez" graphics. My cousin had an Apple IIe which is where I was first introduced to text adventures and Apple computers. It would be many more years before I owned an Apple computer of my own.

In highschool I wasn't sure what I wanted to do for a career. I liked my Psychology class, I liked my art classes, and I liked computers, but I didn't know of a career where I could combined those interests. It wasn't until college when I took a class to help students decide on a major that I learned about Humand Factors. It sounded like a perfect fit. It uses Pyschology to help design better technology based on human needs and capabilities. Unfortunately the school I was attending at the time did not have a Human Factors program.

College

In December of 1991 I got married and moved to Indiana so my wife could finish her nursing degree. Upon complettion of her degree she landed a job at the University of Utah hospital. Her job provided a tuition discount for her spouse so I transferred to the University of Utah where I studied Cognitive Psyhology with Dr. David Strayer. The University of Utah had a program in Human Factors, but it was focused on physical ergonomics, and I was interested in cognitive ergonomics. Fortunately, Dr. Strayer had studied at the University of Illinois which has a great Human Factors program.

The dangers of texting and driving

Dr. Strayer later became one of the first researchers to identify the dangers of texting and driving.

"The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration reported that in 2010 driver distraction was the cause of 18 percent of all fatal crashes – with 3,092 people killed – and crashes resulting in an injury – with 416,000 people wounded." -FCC
